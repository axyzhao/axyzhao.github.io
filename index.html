Personal Projects

Visualizations for carbon removal

In spring 2018, I worked with the Center for Carbon Removal (now Carbon180), a non-profit dedicated to supporting carbon capture, to forecast trends in carbon sequestration technologies. I created data visualizations that mapped the movement of carbon from feedstocks to final products, which were presented by Carbon180 to the Senate Environment and Public Works Committee in 2018.

The above visualization shows the process of converting carbon feedstocks such as agricultural or landfill waste into final products based on a set of processes. This allows us to see gaps and opportunities in the carbon recycling market. The data was gathered over several months by doing comprehensive market research and performing due diligence on different carbon startups. The weights are the relative number of companies working on this particular path.

The map, created on Tableau, shows concentrations of different startups distributed across the US. Where are these companies focused? What are the geographic advantages (geothermal energy, isolation from cities, cheap rent) that help carbon startups thrive?

Quantifying polarization of presidential candidates

During the 2016 election, I gained valuable experience applying quantitative techniques to political questions. As an intern at University of California Irvine's Ethics Center, I worked with Professor Kristen Monroe to research the presidential election through the lens of political psychology. I organized teams of volunteers to perform qualitative analysis on hundreds of interviews from candidates, implemented statistical techniques in Python and R, and analyzed trends on political polarization in the United States.

Applying the concept of integrative complexity from political science, which measures complexity in cognition, I measured each Republican and Democratic candidate based on past interviews. We found that in 2016, Democrats scored higher on the complexity scale in questions of foreign policy, while both parties were low on questions of domestic policy. I presented the results of my research as the lead author of the project (“Integrative Complexity in Election Cycles”) at the International Society of Political Psychology's annual conference.
